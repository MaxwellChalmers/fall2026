## What To Notice

AI systems are often described in terms that make them seem immaterial — algorithms, models, cloud services, digital infrastructure. In practice, they are physical systems. They require chips manufactured from rare earth materials. They run on servers in data centers that consume electricity and water for cooling. Training large models requires energy at a scale comparable to significant industrial processes. The hardware has supply chains; the supply chains have labor; the labor has conditions.

Those costs are real, but they are systematically absent from how AI systems are described and marketed. A company announcing a new AI capability does not typically describe the energy consumption of the training run, the water used to cool the data center, or the conditions in the mines that produced the materials in the hardware. The presentation is all capability and no cost — and that absence is not accidental. Making the material costs visible would change the terms on which people evaluate the technology.

Look for: systems described primarily in terms of their outputs (accuracy, speed, capability) with no account of their resource consumption; systems deployed in contexts where energy or water costs fall on communities that don't benefit from the system's outputs; systems where the physical infrastructure — hardware, data centers, cabling — is concentrated in ways that create dependencies or extract value from particular places.

## Questions To Ask

- What physical resources does this system consume, and where do they come from?
- What are the energy and water costs of this system's operation?
- Where is the system's infrastructure physically located, and who bears the local costs of that infrastructure?
- Who benefits from the system's outputs, and who bears the environmental and labor costs of its operation?
- Are those costs disclosed, and to whom?
- What would the system's cost-benefit analysis look like if material costs were included?

## Why This Matters

This card is the observational counterpart to the STS concept "AI is material" — where that card explains why materiality matters analytically, this card tells you what to look for in a specific deployed system.

Use it when a system is being evaluated primarily on technical performance metrics, and ask what the accounting looks like when physical costs are included. It is also useful for connecting infrastructure concentration (the "Power can concentrate in infrastructure" card) to the question of who bears the hidden costs of that concentration.
