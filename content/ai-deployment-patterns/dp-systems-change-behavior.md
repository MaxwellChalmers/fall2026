## What To Notice

AI systems that flag, score, classify, or intervene change how the people they act on behave over time. A recidivism score affects what a person does after release. A content moderation system changes what people post. A credit score shapes financial decisions. A workplace monitoring system changes how employees work. These effects are sometimes by design — the whole point is to change behavior. But they also happen in ways that weren't anticipated, and those unanticipated changes feed back into the data the system collects.

Look for: evidence that the system's outputs have changed the behavior of the people it acts on. Watch for three patterns in particular. Compliance: people conform to what the system rewards, which may or may not align with the underlying goal the system was built to serve. Avoidance: people change their behavior to stay under the threshold, which means the system is now measuring something different from what it was built to detect. Gaming: people optimize for the score itself rather than the underlying behavior — a pattern well-documented in credit scoring, academic metrics, and recidivism tools. In each case, the training data becomes a poor model of the population the system is now acting on.

Watch also for resistance: when people push back against a system's classifications, that pushback is itself information about whether the system's categories are working. It is often invisible in the system's outputs.

## Questions To Ask

- How have people responded to being classified or scored by this system?
- Is the behavioral change compliance, avoidance, gaming, or resistance?
- Has the system changed the behavior it was designed to detect or predict?
- Does the system's model account for the behavioral changes the system itself produces?
- What happens when behavioral change makes the original training data obsolete?
- Are people optimizing for the score rather than the underlying goal the score was meant to track?

## Why This Matters

This card describes the observational pattern corresponding to the STS concept "Systems are reactive: they change what they measure." It is also the downstream complement to "Prediction imports the past" — the past gets imported, the system acts on it, and that action changes what the future looks like.

Use this card when a system is claimed to predict, detect, or assess something stable — behavior, risk, performance — and ask whether the system's interventions have already reshaped what they are supposedly measuring. Systems that score, rank, or monitor over time are especially likely to show this pattern, because the people being measured have more time and more information to respond to the system's presence.
